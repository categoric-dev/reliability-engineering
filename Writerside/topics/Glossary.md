# Glossary

Epistemology
: The study of knowledge: how we acquire truth, validate evidence, and structure understanding.*

Empiricism
: The theory that knowledge is derived primarily from sensory experience and `evidence`.

Rationalism
: The philosophical view that reason and logic are the primary sources of knowledge, independent of sensory experience.

Falsifiability
: The requirement that for a `hypothesis` to be scientific, it must be framed in a way that allows it to be proven false through empirical testing.

Hypothesis
: A proposed explanation made on the basis of limited evidence as a starting point for further investigation.

Evidence
: The available body of facts or information indicating whether a belief or proposition is true or valid.

Tacit Knowledge
: Subjective, internalized knowledge‚Äîsuch as intuition and experience‚Äîthat is difficult to articulate or codify.

Explicit Knowledge
: Knowledge that has been articulated, codified, and stored in a formal medium, making it transferable.

Knowledge‚Äëmethodology
: A structured framework that systematically captures, organizes, and validates `explicit knowledge` and `tacit knowledge` to guide decision‚Äëmaking through the use of `models`.

Model
: An abstract representation of a system that captures its essential properties for the purpose of analysis or prediction.

Discovery‚Äëoriented Process
: An iterative `knowledge‚Äëmethodology` that prioritizes exploration, `hypothesis` testing, and continuous learning over predetermined sequences.

System Theory
: A theoretical framework asserting that the truth of a system is found in its `relationships`, `feedback loops`, and `context` rather than in isolated components.

Relationships
: The connections or interactions between two or more elements within a system.

Feedback Loops
: Processes where the output of a system is circled back as an input, either amplifying (positive) or dampening (negative) the system's behavior.

Context
: The circumstances, environment, and conditions that surround a system and influence its operation.

System Thinking
: A cognitive approach that shifts focus from `reductionism` to holistic problem‚Äësolving by analyzing how changes propagate through a structure.

Reductionism
: The practice of analyzing a complex system by breaking it down into its smallest constituent parts, often ignoring the interactions between them.

Systems Engineering
: An interdisciplinary field that applies `system thinking` and a `discovery‚Äëoriented process` to design and manage complex systems over their `life cycles`.

Life Cycles
: The series of stages a system passes through, from initial concept and design through operation to retirement.

Reliability Engineering
: A sub-discipline of `systems engineering` that systematically analyzes failure risks to ensure the system achieves `robustness`.

## Ethics & Axiology
*The study of value: determining what is "good," "right," or "purposeful."*

Axiology
: The overarching philosophical study of value, encompassing both ethics (moral value) and aesthetics.

Teleology
: The study of purpose or design; the belief that a system is defined by its end goal or ultimate function.

Purpose‚ÄëDriven Design & Integration
: A `teleological` approach where systems are designed specifically to meet an objective in `context`, ensuring all `stakeholder` values are integrated.

Stakeholder
: Any individual, group, or entity that has a vested interest in the outcome or operation of a system.

Holism & Interdependence
: The principle that the whole is more than the sum of its parts; it asserts that the value of a system lies in the `interdependence` of its elements.

Interdependence
: A mutual reliance between two or more elements, where the state of one affects the state of others.

Emergence & Complexity
: The phenomenon where new properties arise from interactions that cannot be predicted by analyzing parts in isolation; it defines a system's `complexity`.

Complexity
: The quality of a system having many interconnected parts and diverse interactions, making its behavior non-linear and difficult to predict.

Robustness
: A value-state where a system maintains its performance and safety despite `perturbations`.

Perturbations
: External disturbances or internal fluctuations that threaten the stability of a system.

Deontology
: An ethical framework that evaluates morality based on adherence to rules, duties, and obligations, regardless of the outcome.

Consequentialism
: An ethical theory suggesting that the morality of an action is judged solely by its outcomes (e.g., Utilitarianism).

Virtue Ethics
: A moral philosophy emphasizing the inherent character and professional excellence of the practitioner rather than specific rules.

Value Alignment
: The process of ensuring a system's operational behaviors are consistent with the ethical values and intentions of its `stakeholders`.

Equity & Justice
: The principle of fairness in how risks, benefits, and burdens are distributed across different user groups within a system.

Moral Agency
: The capacity of an entity to make moral judgments and be held accountable for its actions.

Integrity (Systemic)
: The state of being whole and consistent; it refers to both the technical `robustness` of a system and the ethical consistency of its design process.
